Example 7: Maximum Power Transfer Theorem

Objective:

To investigate and verify that maximum power is delivered to a load resistor when the load resistance RLOAD is equal to the Thevenin resistance RTH of the circuit.

The maximum power transfer theorem states that maximum power is delivered to the load when the resistance of the load RLOAD is equal to the Thevenin equivalent resistance RTH of the circuit supplying the power. This is an essential concept in electrical engineering, particularly in designing circuits for efficient power delivery.

When a circuit is simplified using Thevenin’s theorem, it consists of:

  • Thevenin equivalent voltage source ( VTH ) : This is the open-circuit voltage seen from the load terminals.
  • Thevenin equivalent resistance ( RTH ) : This is the resistance seen by the load when all independent voltage sources are replaced by short circuits and current sources by open circuits.

Theory:
The power delivered to the load RTH is given by the following formula:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} P_{\mathrm{LOAD}} &= \frac{V_{\mathrm{TH}}^2}{\left(R_{\mathrm{TH}} + R_{\mathrm{LOAD}}\right)^2} \cdot R_{\mathrm{LOAD}} \\ \end{aligned} \end{split}\]
  • VTH: Thevenin equivalent voltage (constant).
  • RTH: Thevenin equivalent resistance (constant).
  • RLOAD: Load resistance (variable).

The goal is to calculate and plot the power delivered to RTH for different values of RTH, and show that maximum power is transferred when RLOAD = RTH.

Power Calculation:
For each value of RLOAD, calculate the power delivered to the load using the formula:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} P_{\mathrm{LOAD}} &= \frac{V_{\mathrm{TH}}^2}{\left(R_{\mathrm{TH}} + R_{\mathrm{LOAD}}\right)^2} \cdot R_{\mathrm{LOAD}} \\ \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Calculate the power for different values of RTH, ranging from 0.1×RTH to 10×RTH.

We can observe that the power peaks when RTH = RLOAD .

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import numpy as np

# Given values from previous simulation or manually assigned
V_TH = thevenin_voltage  # Thevenin voltage calculated earlier
R_TH = thevenin_resistance  # Thevenin resistance calculated earlier (600 Ω)

# Range of load resistances (60 Ohm to 6000 Ohms)
R_LOAD_values = np.linspace(60, 6000, 500)  # 500 values between 60Ω and 6000Ω

# Calculate the power delivered to the load for each R_LOAD
power_values = (V_TH**2 * R_LOAD_values) / (R_TH + R_LOAD_values)**2

# Plot the power as a function of load resistance
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.plot(R_LOAD_values, power_values, label='Power Delivered to Load', color='b', linewidth=2)

Part 3: Questions

Why does maximum power occur when RTH = RLOAD?

The Maximum Power Transfer Theorem states that maximum power is delivered to a load when the load resistance RLOAD matches the Thevenin resistance RTH of the source.

Here's why this happens:

The power delivered to the load is given by:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} P_{\mathrm{LOAD}} &= \frac{V_{\mathrm{TH}}^2}{\left(R_{\mathrm{TH}} + R_{\mathrm{LOAD}}\right)^2} \cdot R_{\mathrm{LOAD}} \\ \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Where VTH is the Thevenin equivalent voltage, RTH is the Thevenin equivalent resistance, and RLOAD is the load resistance.

When RLOAD = RTH, the denominator in the equation is minimized, maximizing the overall power delivered to the load.

If RLOAD is too small or too large, the voltage drop across the load decreases, and either too much power is dissipated in RTH (if RLOAD is too small) or the current decreases (if RLOAD is too large), leading to less power being delivered to the load.

In essence, when the load and source resistances are matched, the circuit is impedance-matched, which allows maximum energy transfer from the source to the load.

Mathematical Proof of the Maximum Power Transfer Theorem

1. Power Delivered to the Load:

The power delivered to the load PLOAD is given by the following formula:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} P_{\mathrm{LOAD}} &= I_{\mathrm{LOAD}}^2 \cdot R_{\mathrm{LOAD}} \\ \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Where I LOAD is the current through the load resistor.

From Ohm's law, the current in the circuit is:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} I_{\mathrm{LOAD}} &= \frac{V_{\mathrm{TH}}}{R_{\mathrm{TH}} + R_{\mathrm{LOAD}}} \\ \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Substitute this into the power formula:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} P_{\mathrm{LOAD}} &= \left( \frac{V_{\mathrm{TH}}}{R_{\mathrm{TH}} + R_{\mathrm{LOAD}}} \right)^2 \cdot R_{\mathrm{LOAD}} \\ \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

2. Differentiating Power with Respect to RLOAD:

To find the condition for maximum power, we differentiate PLOAD with respect to RLOAD and set the derivative equal to 0:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \frac{d}{dR_{\mathrm{LOAD}}} \left( \frac{V_{\mathrm{TH}}^2 \cdot R_{\mathrm{LOAD}}}{(R_{\mathrm{TH}} + R_{\mathrm{LOAD}})^2} \right) &= 0 \\ \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

3. Applying the Quotient Rule:

Let f(RLOAD) = VTH2 · RLOAD and g(RLOAD) = (RTH + RLOAD)2, so the power can be rewritten as:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} P_{\mathrm{LOAD}} &= \frac{f(R_{\mathrm{LOAD}})}{g(R_{\mathrm{LOAD}})} \\ \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Using the quotient rule for differentiation:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \frac{d}{dR_{\mathrm{LOAD}}} \left( \frac{f(R_{\mathrm{LOAD}})}{g(R_{\mathrm{LOAD}})} \right) &= \frac{f'(R_{\mathrm{LOAD}}) \cdot g(R_{\mathrm{LOAD}}) - f(R_{\mathrm{LOAD}}) \cdot g'(R_{\mathrm{LOAD}})}{g(R_{\mathrm{LOAD}})^2} \\ \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Now, calculate the derivatives:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} f'(R_{\mathrm{LOAD}}) &= V_{\mathrm{TH}}^2 \\ g'(R_{\mathrm{LOAD}}) &= 2(R_{\mathrm{TH}} + R_{\mathrm{LOAD}}) \\ \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Substitute these into the quotient rule formula:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \frac{dP_{\mathrm{LOAD}}}{dR_{\mathrm{LOAD}}} &= \frac{V_{\mathrm{TH}}^2 \cdot (R_{\mathrm{TH}} + R_{\mathrm{LOAD}})^2 - V_{\mathrm{TH}}^2 \cdot R_{\mathrm{LOAD}} \cdot 2(R_{\mathrm{TH}} + R_{\mathrm{LOAD}})}{(R_{\mathrm{TH}} + R_{\mathrm{LOAD}})^4} \\ \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

4. Conclusion:

For maximum power, set the derivative equal to 0:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} R_{\mathrm{TH}}^2 - R_{\mathrm{LOAD}}^2 &= 0 \\ \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

This gives:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} R_{\mathrm{TH}} &= R_{\mathrm{LOAD}} \\ \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Thus, the power delivered to the load is maximized when:

\[ \begin{aligned} R_{\mathrm{TH}} &= R_{\mathrm{LOAD}} \end{aligned} \]

What happens to the power delivered when RLOAD is much larger or smaller than RTH ?

When RLOAD is much smaller than RTH :

If the load resistance RLOAD is much smaller than the Thevenin resistance RTH, most of the voltage will drop across RTH, and only a small portion of the voltage will drop across RLOAD .

This results in a higher current, but because the voltage drop across RLOAD is small, the power delivered to the load is also small .

In this case, the circuit behaves as if it is "shorted," and most of the power is dissipated in the internal resistance RTH, not in the load .

When RLOAD is much larger than RTH :

If the load resistance RLOAD is much larger than the Thevenin resistance RTH, the current in the circuit will be very small, since total resistance increases significantly .

Even though most of the voltage will drop across RLOAD , the small current results in less power delivered to the load.

The circuit behaves as if it is "open" or "highly resistive," causing minimal power transfer .

Conclusion :

In both cases (too small or too large RLOAD), the power delivered to the load is less than the maximum, which occurs when RLOAD = RTH .
